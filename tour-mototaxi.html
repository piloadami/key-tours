The mototaxi and the city

Mototaxis became essential in Rio in the 1990s, filling the gaps that buses and cars couldn't reach — the steep hillside communities, the narrow lanes threading between houses, the shortcuts that only locals know. In a city built on mountains and valleys, the motorcycle is often the only vehicle that makes sense. Today there are thousands of mototaxi drivers in Rio, each one a navigator of a neighbourhood, a keeper of a mental map that no GPS has fully captured.

For most visitors, the mototaxi remains invisible — something seen from the window of a taxi, something that zips past on a hill. Getting on one changes your entire relationship with the city. The scale collapses. Streets that looked like urban sprawl from a distance become distinct neighbourhoods with their own rhythm. You feel the gradient of the hills, the texture of the roads, the temperature change as you rise above the city.

The routes no tourist sees

Keyla has been navigating Rio on motos since she was a teenager. She knows the spots that offer the best unobstructed view of both the sea and the bay at once. She knows the drivers — the ones who have been working the same streets for twenty years, who know every pothole and every shortcut, who stop without being asked when something is worth seeing.

This tour moves through parts of the city that are simply inaccessible by car: the narrow lanes of hillside communities, the old roads that cut behind the mountains, the spots where Rio reveals itself suddenly — a gap between houses and there's the full bay, or a corner turned and there's the Christ above you, closer than you expected.

"The moto tour is the one people talk about afterwards. It's not comfortable in the way a hotel is comfortable — it's the kind of thing that makes you feel alive. Hold on, lean with the turns, and just watch the city happen around you."
— Keyla, your guide
