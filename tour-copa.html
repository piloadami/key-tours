Copacabana

More than a beach. A whole chapter of Rio's history. Mais do que uma praia. Um capítulo inteiro da história carioca. Más que una playa. Un capítulo entero de la historia de Río.

Before the tunnel, Copacabana didn't exist — or rather, it was a fishing village completely cut off from the city by a solid mountain. The Túnel Velho, blasted through the rock and completed in 1892, changed everything. Within a generation, one of the world's most mythologised addresses was born.

The iconic black-and-white wave mosaic boardwalk — the calçadão — was designed in 1970 by Roberto Burle Marx, Brazil's most celebrated landscape architect. The pattern, inspired by the undulating hills of Lisbon's Rossio Square, became the visual shorthand for Rio itself. You've seen it in a thousand photographs. Walking it with a local who grew up here is something else entirely.

The neighbourhood behind the postcard

You can begin to understand a little of Copacabana's history through its architecture — in many buildings it has been remarkably preserved.

The neighbourhood carries more cultural history per square metre than almost anywhere else in Brazil. This tour is for people who want to arrive somewhere — not just visit it.

"Copacabana is misunderstood. Everyone sees the same kilometre of beach. But walk two blocks inland and you find a completely different city. I know which bakery has the best pão de queijo in Rio — I've been going there since before I can remember."
— Keyla, your guide
