Into Tijuca Forest, the lungs of the city. Dentro da Floresta da Tijuca, o pulmão da cidade. Dentro de la Floresta de Tijuca, el pulmón de la ciudad.

There is a rainforest inside Rio de Janeiro. Not near it, not beside it — inside it, wrapped around the mountain spine that runs through the city's heart. Tijuca Forest covers 32 square kilometres of Atlantic rainforest, making it one of the largest urban forests in the world. And it shouldn't exist at all.

The forest that humans destroyed — and rebuilt

In the 17th and 18th centuries these mountains were stripped bare for sugar and coffee plantations. By the 1840s Rio de Janeiro was running out of water — the rivers had dried up and the city was in crisis. Emperor Dom Pedro II made a remarkable decision: replant the entire mountain range. Between 1861 and 1874, under the direction of Major Manuel Gomes Archer, workers replanted the slopes by hand with native Atlantic Forest species. The gamble worked. The rivers returned. The city's water supply recovered. The forest grew.

Today Tijuca shelters over 300 bird species, jaguarundi, ocelots, golden lion tamarins, and hundreds of orchid varieties. The Cascatinha Taunay waterfall — painted by European landscape artists in the 1800s — still flows through the same valley. And from the summit of Pico da Tijuca, at 1,021 metres, you can see the full sweep of the city laid out below: Guanabara Bay, the beaches, the islands, the horizon.

The city hiding inside the forest

What most visitors miss is that the forest is not just nature — it's memory. The ruins of old coffee fazendas are still visible on certain trails. The Mayrink Chapel in the forest interior contains panels painted by Cândido Portinari, Brazil's most celebrated 20th-century artist. The forest roads were designed as a 19th-century leisure circuit for the Imperial court — a Sunday drive through the wilderness. You're walking through a landscape shaped by three different centuries of human intention.

"I grew up hiking these trails with my family. My uncle knows every waterfall. I can take you to places that don't appear on any tourist map — paths where you hear the city disappear completely within five minutes of walking."
— Keyla, your guide

A different side of Rio

Most visitors to Rio never make it into the forest. It's one of the best-kept secrets of the city — an hour from the beach, a world away. Keyla grew up here. She knows which trails have the best waterfall swimming, where to watch for toucans at dawn, and which viewpoint gives you a angle of Christ the Redeemer that nobody photographs.
