Rio de Janeiro was born in this neighbourhood. The Portuguese settled on the shores of Guanabara Bay in 1565, and for three centuries the Centro Histórico was the nerve centre of colonial Brazil — the port where enslaved Africans arrived, the square where the Emperor held court, the streets where capoeira was practiced in secret beneath the gaze of the church.

A city written in stone

The Paço Imperial still stands at Praça XV — the palace where Portuguese royalty lived after fleeing Napoleon in 1808, and where Brazil's Independence was proclaimed in 1822. The Carioca Aqueduct, two rows of elegant stone arches completed in 1750, once carried water from the Santa Teresa hills down to the city. Today it carries the iconic yellow tram. Walk through the old financial district and you'll find Belle Époque facades sitting beside Modernist concrete towers beside colonial churches — five hundred years of ambition compressed into a few walkable blocks.

The hill that escaped time

Santa Teresa is a neighbourhood of escaped time. The bohemian hill above the city where artists, poets, and musicians settled throughout the 20th century — drawn by the views, the cool air, and the sense that the rules of the city below didn't quite apply up here. Its cobbled streets wind past colonial mansions now painted terracotta and mustard, past small studios and neighbourhood bars, past mirantes where the whole sweep of the bay opens up below you.

The neighbourhood has roots in Rio's religious history — Carmelite monks built a hermitage here in the 17th century. Later it became home to the city's bohemian intellectual class, and today it remains one of the few places in Rio where you feel both inside the city and completely removed from it.

"Santa Teresa is where I take people when I want them to understand Rio — not the postcard version, the real one. There's a bar I've been going to since I was a teenager. The view from Parque das Ruínas — you see everything from there. I always end up staying longer than planned."
— Keyla, your guide
